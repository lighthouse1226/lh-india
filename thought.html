From the Staff: More Practical Thoughts on Stress Management

Eric Springer Uncategorized

“The greatest weapon against stress is our ability to choose one thought over another.”

 –William James

This is part two of an ongoing series on managing stress in the workplace. Specifically, helpful strategies to keep on track and DELIVER when time or resources are short!

For this entry, I reached out to my colleague Anthony for input. He works in Publishing, where tight deadlines are a regular occurrence. He offered the following, which I agree with wholeheartedly.

 

Lie to Yourself

When your workload starts to boil over the edge of the pot, giving into stress is the last thing you want to do. You’re only adding more fuel to the fire at that point. Instead, turn down the heat. Close down your tasks and take five to get some water or a snack. Que sera, sera. Whatever happens today, it’s out of your control. That couldn’t be further from the truth, of course: your company needs you. But that’s the mental state you must attain during your break. Lie to yourself.

Next, take a look at your calendar (or task management tool) and ask yourself what’s the bare minimum that needs doing this week? What’s the bare minimum that needs doing today? To stay on that schedule, what needs to happen before lunch? At that point, you should see the matrix open itself to you. As it turns out, you’re not under that much pressure. You could literally sit there for two hours twiddling your thumbs and you’d still be on schedule.

Now, take a long swig of your favorite beverage and get to work. You’re gonna get a hell of a lot more than the bare minimum done today.

 

Keep Calm

This one comes from yours truly, backing up Anthony’s advice above. Keeping a cool head has served me well, both professionally and personally. When a high-stress situation confronts me, I work to determine what needs my immediate attention, and what can wait. If it can wait, I put it aside and stop thinking about it. In a way, it’s a lot like denial, or putting blinders on.

 

“It doesn’t look like anything to me.”

This purposeful denial has become one of my most valuable strategies to cope with stressful situations, by allowing me to Keep Calm.

Calling it denial is a bit of hyperbole. In essence, I’m focusing on what I can control. I selectively categorize what thoughts are helpful and what are not, and I push the thoughts that would add further stress, or grind me down to a halt, and choose not to engage them. If I had to break it down I would state it as follows:

  1. Realize too much stress isn’t helpful (and could be damaging)
  2. Determine the appropriate urgency of the situation
  3. Acknowledge that the only path to full stress relief is to find a solution
  4. Write a list and begin work
